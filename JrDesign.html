Self Navigating Robotic Vehicle

In my Junior Design course, I and three of my classmates were tasked with designing, programming, and building a self-navigating robotic vehicle. The vehicle was required to follow colored lanes, avoid wall collisions, and communicate with a central server.

There were two main paths the vehicle needed to follow: track A and track B. Track A is depicted below, and the only difference between track A and track B is that track B follows the blue path. The vehicle was required to complete these tracks both solo, and in tandem with a partner group. This tasked both groups to plan ahead for collisions to ensure that each group's vehicle would not interfere with the other's path.

The project was divided into two main components: hardware and software. The hardware component involved an H-Bridge for motor control, infrared sensors for collision detection, and phototransistors for color sensing. A 3D-printed chassis was also designed to ensure efficient component placement on our vehicle. The software component involved embedded C programming on an Arduino UNO microcontroller and the implementation of a WebSocket client for real-time communication between vehicles. An OLED display was also included to provide visual feedback on the vehicle's state.

H-Bridge Motor Control: An H-Bridge is a circuit we used to control the direction and speed of the DC motors on our vehicle. It allowed us to reverse the polarity of the voltage applied to the motors without any risk of damaging the components. The H-bridge connects to PWM pins on the Arduino, allowing us to control the speed and direction of the motors.

Collision Detection: A phototransistor and an infrared LED were attached to the front of the vehicle for collision detection. The light that the LED emits is reflected off of walls and detected by the phototransistor. The voltage across the phototransistor is dependent on the amount of reflected light, and if under a specified threshold, instruct the vehicle to turn away from the wall.

Lane following: Two photodiodes were attached to each side of the vehicle to act as its eyes. Next to the photodiodes were red and blue LEDs. These would flash on and off, and the photodiodes would detect the amount of light reflected off the ground. Then, depending on what path the vehicle was on, the Arduino would follow pre-determined logic to steer the vehicle in the correct direction.

OLED Screen: The OLED screen proved to be a helpful addition in not only debugging but also providing an asthetic touch to the vehicle. The screen displays what track the vehicle is following at the beginning of the run, and then displays the vehicle's current objective as it moves through the track.

All of these components, except for the motors, were powered by a 9V battery. It proved to be a reliable power source for the components that were not incredibly power hungry. The motors, however, were powered by a 6V battery pack. Although at a smaller voltage, the motors were able to draw more current from the pack and not lose power nearly as quickly.
